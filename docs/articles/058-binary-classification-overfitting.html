This article explains classification, decision boundaries, and overfitting from scratch — zero jargon, plenty of pictures. If you can sort laundry into darks and lights, you already understand the core idea.

1 — What is Classification?

Classification is sorting things into bins. That is it. You do it every day:

  • Email → Spam or Not Spam (2 bins)
  • Photo → Cat or Dog (2 bins)
  • Medical scan → Healthy or Needs-attention (2 bins)

When there are exactly two bins, we call it binary classification. The computer looks at the data (numbers describing each item) and decides: Bin A or Bin B.

But how does it decide? It draws a line.

2 — The Decision Boundary (The Line on the Map)

Imagine a map with two countries. A border runs between them — everything north of the border is Country A, everything south is Country B. That border is the decision boundary.

In classification, the computer draws exactly this kind of border through the data. Points on one side get labeled Class A; points on the other side get Class B.

    Quiz 2
    score
      |
   10 |  x         x            x = Pass (Class A)
      |     x    x               o = Fail (Class B)
    7 | - - - - - - - - -  ← decision boundary
      |  o     o
    4 |     o        o
      |  o
    1 |________________________
      1    3    5    7    9   Quiz 1 score

Everything above the line → predicted Pass. Everything below → predicted Fail. The line IS the decision. The question is: what shape should that line be?

3 — Straight Lines vs Curvy Lines

The shape of the boundary depends on how much flexibility we give the computer. Think of it as choosing a tool to draw with:

Degree 1 — Ruler (straight line)

The simplest possible boundary. One straight cut through the data. Works when the two groups sit on opposite sides neatly — but if the data curves, a straight line will misclassify points on both ends.

Degree 2 — French curve (one bend)

Now the boundary can curve once, like the letter U or an arc. More flexible — it can wrap around a cluster that a straight line would slice through.

Degree 3-5 — Freehand squiggle (many wiggles)

The boundary can twist and turn to dodge around individual data points. It fits the training data almost perfectly. Sounds great — but this is where trouble starts.

  DEGREE 1 (straight)       DEGREE 3 (wiggly)

    |  x  x                   |  x  x
    |  x     x                |  x /   x
    | --------\---            |  /  \   \
    |  o  o    \              | o  o \   |
    |     o     o             |    o  \--/  o
    |________________________ |________________________

    misses 2 points           hits every point
    but the LINE makes sense  but the SHAPE is suspicious

The straight line gets a couple of points wrong, but you can see the general trend. The wiggly line nails every training point — but the twists exist only to chase individual dots, not because the real pattern is that complicated.

4 — Overfitting: The Student Who Memorized Too Hard

This is the single most important concept in machine learning. Here is the best way to think about it:

Imagine a student preparing for a math exam.

Underfitting = the student barely studied. Glanced at the textbook once. On exam day, they cannot solve even the easy problems. Their "model" of math is too simple — a straight line through curved data.

Overfitting = the student memorized every practice problem and its answer, word for word. They ace the practice test with a perfect score. But on the real exam, where questions are phrased slightly differently, they freeze. They did not learn math — they learned specific answers.

Just right = the student understood the underlying principles. They can solve new problems they have never seen before, because they learned the pattern, not the examples.

In classification terms:

Boundary shapeTraining accuracyNew data accuracy
UnderfittingToo straight (degree 1 for curved data)LowLow
Just rightMatches the real pattern (degree 2-3)GoodGood
OverfittingToo wiggly (degree 5 chasing noise)PerfectPoor

The trap: overfitting looks like success because it scores perfectly on data you already have. It only fails when new, unseen data arrives — which is the only data that matters in the real world.

5 — Too Rigid vs Too Flexible

There is a fundamental tension in every model:

  • Too rigid (low degree) — the model cannot capture the real pattern. It ignores the data and draws the same boring straight line no matter what. It is consistently wrong in a predictable way.
  • Too flexible (high degree) — the model captures everything, including random noise and measurement errors. It is wildly different every time you train it on slightly different data, because it is chasing noise instead of signal.

The sweet spot is in the middle: flexible enough to capture the real shape of the data, rigid enough to ignore the random wobble.

  Error on
  new data

Start too rigid → errors are high because the model is too simple. Increase flexibility → errors drop as the model starts matching real patterns. Keep increasing → errors rise again because the model is memorizing noise. The best model lives at the bottom of that valley.

6 — Worked Example: 10 Students, Pass or Fail

Ten students took two quizzes. Blue dots passed the course; red dots failed. Here is the data on a 2D plot:

    Quiz 2
      |
   10 |        B                 B = passed (blue)
    9 |  B           B          R = failed (red)
    8 |     B     B
    7 |
    6 |  R              R
    5 |
    4 |     R     R
    3 |  R
    2 |
      |________________________
      1  2  3  4  5  6  7  8   Quiz 1

Degree 1 (straight line)

The computer draws a horizontal line at about Quiz 2 = 7. Everything above → Pass. Everything below → Fail.

      |
   10 |        B                  ↑ Pass zone
    9 |  B           B
    8 |     B     B
    7 | ========================  ← boundary
    6 |  R              R         ↓ Fail zone
    4 |     R     R
    3 |  R
      |________________________

  Result: 10/10 correct on this data!
  Clean, simple, easy to trust.

Degree 5 (wiggly line)

The computer draws a curve that snakes between every single point, hugging each one individually:

      |
   10 |        B
    9 |  B  /--+  \   B
    8 |   B/    B  \-/
    7 |  /            \
    6 | / R     ,---.  R\
    5 ||   \   /     \   |
    4 |  \  R-+   R   |  |
    3 |  R\          /
    2 |    \________/
      |________________________

  Result: 10/10 correct on training data!
  But look at that snake. If one new student
  scores (5, 7), the model might say FAIL
  because the curve dips there — even though
  (5, 7) clearly belongs with the passers.

Both models score 10/10 on the training data. But only the straight line will work well on new students. The wiggly line memorized the specific ten students instead of learning the real rule ("score above ~7 on Quiz 2 and you pass").

7 — Try It Yourself

Open the interactive widget and drag the polynomial degree slider from 1 to 5. Watch how the boundary goes from a clean line to a tangled mess:

Open Binary Classification Widget →

  1. Set degree to 1 — notice the straight boundary. Some points may be on the wrong side.
  2. Set degree to 2 — the boundary bends. More points land on the correct side.
  3. Set degree to 5 — the boundary twists wildly. Every training point is correct, but the shape makes no intuitive sense.
  4. Ask yourself: if I added one new data point, which boundary would I trust to classify it correctly?

The answer is almost always degree 1 or 2. The simplest boundary that fits the data well enough is the one that generalizes to new, unseen data.

8 — Key Takeaways

#Lesson
1Classification = sorting into bins. Binary classification = exactly two bins.
2Decision boundary = the dividing line. Its shape determines how the model sorts new items.
3More flexibility is not always better. A wiggly boundary fits old data perfectly but fails on new data.
4Overfitting = memorizing, not learning. The model aces the practice test but fails the real exam.
5Underfitting = not studying enough. The model is too simple to capture even the obvious pattern.
6The sweet spot is "just flexible enough" — capturing the real pattern while ignoring noise.
