Anomaly Detection & Dimensionality Reduction

unsupervised-learning anomaly-detection dimensionality-reduction chula-class ✔ solid (class material + real-world examples)

Source: Chula AI Class (Unsupervised Learning subtypes) — deep-dive companion to Article 057

Two powerful techniques that don't need labeled data. One finds the weird stuff. The other throws away the noise.

Part A — Anomaly Detection: Finding the Odd One Out

What is it?

Imagine a basket with 99 apples and 1 orange. Anomaly detection is the model that looks at the basket and says: "That orange doesn't belong."

How: the model learns what "normal" looks like from the bulk of the data, then flags anything too far outside that pattern. Think of it like drawing a circle around where most data lives — inside = normal, way outside = anomaly.

The key insight: you don't need labels. Nobody hand-tags each transaction as "fraud" or "not fraud." The model figures out normal on its own.

Example 1: Credit Card Fraud

Your card has 6 months of history. The model learns: typical spend 500–2,000 baht, location Bangkok, time 8am–11pm.

Then one night: 50,000 baht in Lagos, Nigeria at 3:17am.

Three dimensions off at once (amount, location, time). The model doesn't know the word "fraud" — it just knows this looks nothing like your behavior. Flag it.

Example 2: Network Security (DDoS)

A server normally handles 100 requests/second (range: 60–150). Suddenly: 50,000 requests/second from 10,000 IPs. That's 500x normal — the model flags it as a probable DDoS attack.

Example 3: Factory Predictive Maintenance

A motor's vibration sensor reads 0.5–1.2 Hz for a year. One morning: 4.5 Hz (3–4x normal). Flag. An engineer finds a failing bearing — early repair costs 3,000 baht vs. 150,000 baht + 2 days downtime if it seizes.

Why unsupervised?

You never told the model what to look for. You just showed it thousands of normal data points: "Learn what normal looks like. Tell me when something isn't."

Part B — Dimensionality Reduction: Keeping What Matters

What is it?

Imagine describing a person with 50 attributes: height, weight, shoe size, eye color, hair length, favorite food, blood type... But to predict whether they'd enjoy a sci-fi movie, most of those don't matter.

Dimensionality reduction boils 50 attributes down to the 3–5 that actually matter.

Why reduce?

ReasonExplanation
Speed20 features runs dramatically faster than 1,000
NoiseShoe size doesn't predict income — including it confuses the model
OverfittingToo many dimensions + limited data = memorizing noise, not learning patterns
VisualizationCan't plot 50 dimensions, but can plot 2–3

The Netflix Example

Netflix tracks ~1,000 behavioral signals per user: watch time per genre, time of day, intro skip rate, series completion rate, ratings, pause frequency, rewatch rate...

A model on 1,000 raw features would be slow and noisy. So they reduce to ~20 "taste dimensions":

  • "Likes fast-paced action" (combines action watch time + completion + rewatch)
  • "Prefers short episodes" (episode length preference + skip patterns)
  • "Late-night binge watcher" (time-of-day + session length)

These 20 capture ~95% of useful information. The other 5%? Noise.

The Curse of Dimensionality

More dimensions = you need exponentially more data to fill the space.

In 1D (a line), 10 points cover it well. In 2D (a square), you need 10×10 = 100. In 3D (a cube), 10×10×10 = 1,000. By dimension 50? You'd need 1050 points — more than atoms in the Earth.

Your data becomes so sparse that no point is near any other. The model can't find patterns. Dimensionality reduction is the fix: cut to dimensions that matter, and your data is dense enough to learn from.
